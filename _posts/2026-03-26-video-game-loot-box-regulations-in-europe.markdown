## BLUF

Video games are implementing games of chance that effectively make them online casinos at an
increasing rate. European countries are ahead of the United States in their effort to regulate these
actors, with some countries banning loot boxes all-together. Through my 2,500+ hours of experience in
Counter-strike 2, I have a first-hand account of the tactics used by companies to persuade people to buy
loot boxes and build up a game's economic system.

## Background

The popular competitive video game Counter-strike 2 generates hundreds of million dollars
annually for its parent company, Valve, despite the game being free-to-play. This revenue is generated
largely from loot boxes, or "mystery packages of digital content in video games which can be
purchased for small sums and in exchange a random in-game content can be received by the player"
("Loot Box Regulations in the EU"). Valve not only takes all of the profit from this primary method of
purchasing in-game content, but also charge a 15 percent fee on all sales that take place on their
secondary marketplace, meaning they profit on each piece of content multiple times. On top of that,
sales made on this secondary marketplace are converted into what is essentially store credit and cannot
be withdrawn from the marketplace without using unofficial, unreliable third-party sources.

While this situation is alarming as-is, another layer of concern is added once it is understood
that these loot boxes are not protected by any age verification mechanism. Children are easily able to
access these chance-based games, and often do so without the concent or knowledge of their parents.
According to a study done in the UK in 2018, the number of children with gambling problems has
quadrupled to more than 50,000 in two years, and the trend has likely gotten worse since then (The
Conversation). This same predatory pattern is taking place across hundreds, if not thousands, of video
games today. Many are specifically marketed towards underage people. Some countries in Europe and
the EU itself are taking notice.

## Analysis

The list of European countries that either have legislation in place or are working on it includes
the UK, Germany, Spain, Finland, Austria, Poland, and most importantly, the Netherlands and Belgium
("Loot Box Regulations in the EU"). These two nations are taking the most strict approach on the topic.
Belgium has completely banned any type of loot box since 2018, and the Netherlands bans loot boxes
"that the content of these loot boxes is determines by chance and that the prizes to be won can be traded
outside of the game: the prizes have a market value" (Chalk). Additionally, the EU is enacting new
legislation as of June 2026 that requires all games with purchasable loot boxes to have an age rating of
16 or older. This would require all gamers under that age to have a legal adult present if they wanted to
purchase a game with loot boxes (Denzer).

While facing this criticism, Valve has compared its loot box mechanic to that of Pokemon cards
or Baseball cards. They say users being able to trade and sell their items is good for consumers, and
that they enjoy moving from old items to new ones (Manchester). Valve has also been making changes
in-game to prepare for more regulations to be put in place. Users are now given the chance to use
"terminals" instead of loot boxes. These terminals give the user five random choices to choose from
with the option to decline all choices for free. This takes most of the predatory nature out of cases, and
will likely be much less profitable for Valve in the long run.

## Recommendations

This issue is not going away anytime soon. The US needs to catch up with countries like
Belgium in order to prevent children from developing long-term issues at a young age. Personally, I
know many people who have lost significant sums of money on Counter-strike 2 alone when they were
children. The expanse of underage online gambling is not to be taken lightly. Even adults should be
protected by the law to some extent in this matter. The rise of sports gambling, online casinos, and loot
boxes leave too many financial bear traps for people to step in.

One legislation change I believe could work in the United States would be to have ID
verification on games with mature content. The EU's regulation that requires an adult to be present at
purchase falls apart when you realize most video game sales are done online. Kids simply use their
parent's information to sign up for an account and get unfettered access to mature content. With ID
verification, more parents would investigate why such a high level of verification is required before
giving their children unrestricted access.
