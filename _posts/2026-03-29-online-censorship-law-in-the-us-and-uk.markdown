## BLUF

Social media is the place where people, especially young people, get the majority of their news
and decide what they believe in today's world. In the United Kingdom, regulations have been put in
place to help stem the increasing extremism in the general population. The Wild West free speech
approach taken by the United States has caused fractures in unity and cooperation in our political
system and everyday life. We can take lessons from the UK to implement a change that can help reunite
Americans and stop the echo chamber of social media.

## Background

The world is becoming more extreme by the day. People everywhere are less inclined to change
their minds than ever before, even when presented with evidence that contradicts what they believe.
This can be partially attributed to social media, with many people facing dangerous "echo chambers" in
their social media algorithms. Websites like Instagram and TikTok have found that people are likely to
keep scrolling if the content they are shown is easy to digest and does not challenge the users' beliefs.
This results in a user only seeing posts from the sides of issues they agree with, and never hearing the
counter-argument. A snowball-effect can happen, where people are moved to more extreme beliefs
quickly due to what they see on their feed every day (Bouchrika).

It is reasonable to think these social media giants should be regulated in a way that prevents
these negative effects of social media. However, it is incredibly complicated to put into place in the real
world. In the United Kingdom, a law was passed in 2023 called the Online Safety Act. This act deems
some social media content as illegal and places age restrictions on other content (Department for
Science, Innovation, and Technology). Heavy criticism soon followed, warning that the act represents a
step towards an authoritarian regime where free speech is not guaranteed (Konstantinos). This criticism
was in spite of the fact that the act only regulated content that includes:

- child sexual abuse
- controlling or coercive behaviour
- extreme sexual violence
- extreme pornography
- fraud
- racially or religiously aggravated public order offences
- inciting violence
- illegal immigration and people smuggling
- promoting or facilitating suicide
- intimate image abuse
- selling illegal drugs or weapons
- sexual exploitation
- terrorism (Department for Science, Innovation, and Technology).

Content of this type would generally be agreed upon as reasonable topics to restrict, but it is in the
implementation of these regulations that worries the public. What is terrorism to one person may not be
to the next.

## Analysis

While likely drafted and passed in good faith, it is impossible to know the exact effects of this
type of legislation until it is put into practice. After more than two years under the Online Safety Act,
complex implementation efforts have been put into place. Services have been categorized, requiring
different content restriction features for each category. Additionally, more advanced age-verification
measures are required in some cases to ensure compliance with the child protection language in the act.
Criticisms officially listed on the UK parliamentary library's website include its tunnel-vision on
content rather than access to services themselves, third-party organizations reporting an increase in
child sexual abuse content, and its lack of preparedness for AI and the speed at which AI-generated
content can be generated (Woodhouse).

## Recommendations

There has been an ongoing effort to pass the Kids Online Safety Act since 2022. This act would
help reduce harmful content from reaching children under the age of 13 (119th Congress). It is certainly
a step in the right direction to regulate the tech industry.

While this act has the potential to help a portion of the population, it does not reach the same
level of restriction as the United Kingdom's Online Safety Act. Big tech and social media firms do not
see their users as real people, we are simply numbers and bank accounts to them. If they can make
money off of indoctrinating young men into the alt-right pipeline or selling discriminatory tragedies to
young women, they will. The best type of regulation would require content to be recommended or
shown based on the issue itself, not on one side or another. People who show an interest in a topic like
abortion should not be allowed to be categorized into a pro-life or pro-choice box by their algorithm.
The personalization should end at the topic, and opinions from everywhere on the spectrum should be
shown without preference.

This type of change would allow people to form and change their opinions while looking at the
whole picture, rather than hearing only what they want to hear. It is a change that could never happen
without regulation, since a social media company putting this into place would likely be economically
unviable since other companies would be feeding more easily digestible content to users. It has to be all
companies being required to do it at once. Every choice someone makes signals they are part of one
"team" or another. The "with us or against us" mentality has never been stronger, and bitterness and
hatred are rife among people who disagree. A change like this to our social media would throw a life
preserver to the United States and promote rational conversation and respectful disagreement.
