## BLUF

Today's job-seekers are faced with scammers, fraudulent job postings, and inactive job postings
in large numbers when searching for a position online. There are ways to mitigate the frequency of
these issues with the better legislation and an in-depth understanding of the issue.

## Background

Fake job postings are on the rise. A study done by ResumeBuilder shows that three of every ten
companies have fake listings on their careers page or job boards such as Indeed or LinkedIn (Cerullo).
Speculation for the reason behind this phenomenon ranges from representing growth, employee
intimidation, proving a diverse group was "considered", or simply searching for an extraordinary
applicant. This practice is not explicitly illegal in US law, but statutes such as the FTC Act could apply
in some situations. On the other hand, there are people who impersonate companies in order to extract
money or information from victims. Impersonation scams are illegal under US law, but the people that
pose these fraudulent advertisements still often go unpunished due attribution challenges (Handwerker
and Pepper).

An impersonation scam occurs when someone tries to convince you they are someone in
authority when they are not ("Imposter Scams"). This type of scam can take many forms such as
phishing emails, spam texts, or fake recruiters on social media. Often, someone posing as a recruiter
will contact someone looking for a job, offering a lucrative, flexible position. Then, after building trust
through a phone call "interview" or a simple email exchange, the scammer will ask for payment for a
fabricated reason such as account access or technical requirements (Indeed Editorial Team).

This practice has a detrimental effect on the job market from both the employee and employer
perspectives. First, candidates waste time and lose trust in companies that post fake jobs. A candidate
that applied to 15 ghost jobs at a company and did not hear back from any of them would likely be
discouraged from applying to that company in the future. Additionally, candidates' resumes are
changing constantly. A resume collected now will be outdated in six months (McGovern).

## Analysis

For the scenario involving a fake recruiter or company attempting to trick victims into exposing
sensitive information or sending money to the scammer, the law is clear. This is a violation of the Wire
Fraud Statue, or 18 USC 1343 (Cornell Law School). The issue the US faces with this scenario is
applying the law. The amount lost is usually small, making these cases a low priority for investigation.
Additionally, many scammers operate out of foreign countries with advanced barriers to tracking. It is
usually not worth it to track down the scammer for each individual case.

The case where companies are posting ghost jobs is also difficult to mitigate. The Federal Trade
Commission Act prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce" (Federal
Reserve). However, these ghost job postings are seen as a gray area due to the difficulty in proving an
employer's intent to hire (Handwerker and Pepper). The negative affects on job-seekers are also
challenging to quantify in many cases. Most of the time, there is only a loss of time when people follow
along with fake interviews and other similar tasks. Even when there is a transfer of money between the
parties, a similar issue to the first scenario arises. The money lost does not warrant the cost of finding
the perpetrator.

## Recommendations

Some states have put forth legislative proposals to curb these issues. New Jersey proposed a bill
that would require job postings to have a mandatory disclosure of an existing vacancy and provide a
time frame for filling that vacancy. It also would require companies to remove a posting for a newly
filled position within two weeks of that position being filled, including postings on external job boards.
Kentucky closely followed suit with a bill requiring specification of a current opening or anticipation of
a future opening for all job listings.

In response to fake recruiters, nothing of note has been proposed yet. One potential remediation
that could be implemented would be to modify Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act. This
act protects companies like LinkedIn and Indeed from being held liable for scams happening on their
site. Even a partial repeal of this act could give these companies a reason to crack down on the rampant
fraudulent schemes that are flourishing on their websites (Brannon, Valerie C., and Eric N. Holmes).
